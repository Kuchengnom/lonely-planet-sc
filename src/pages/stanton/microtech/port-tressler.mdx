<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Main Concourse" category="practical" image="/images/port-tressler/main-concourse.webp" imageAlt="The bustling main concourse of Port Tressler">

The Main Concourse is Port Tressler's beating heart — a cathedral of commerce and transit where the station's various populations meet, mingle, and mostly avoid each other. The concourse is a long, wide corridor that runs the length of the station's central spine, lined with shops, terminals, and the occasional food vendor who has paid the necessary fees to occupy a corner.

The concourse is always crowded, but never chaotic. microTech's engineers have calculated the optimal flow of foot traffic, and the layout reflects their conclusions. There are no sharp corners where crowds might form. No blind spots where someone might lurk. No dark corners where a deal might go wrong. It is the most unromantic space in the Stanton system, and it is exactly what a weary traveler needs after a three-month haul from the frontier.

The shops are functional: ship parts, basic supplies, overpriced snacks that taste like regret but are better than the alternative. The real action is at the information kiosks, where pilots can check manifests, file flight plans, and — if they're lucky — find a cargo job that doesn't involve moving something questionably legal to somewhere questionably safe.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Refinery Deck" category="practical" image="/images/port-tressler/refinery-deck.webp" imageAlt="The industrial refinery deck of Port Tressler">

The Refinery Deck is where raw materials become usable products — ore is smelted, gases are compressed, and chemicals are separated. It is also, for those who know how to look, one of the most honest places in the system. The Refinery Deck doesn't care about your past, your allegiances, or your credit rating. It cares about throughput, purity, and whether your cargo is worth the processing fees.

The deck is a maze of pipes, tanks, and processing units that hum with a frequency that you feel in your teeth. It is loud, hot, and perpetually coated in a fine layer of metallic dust that no cleaning protocol has ever fully removed. Workers on the Refinery Deck wear respirators as standard — not because the air is toxic, but because the cumulative effects of inhaling refined metal particulates are well-documented and unpleasant.

For merchants, the Refinery Deck is where the real money is made. Raw ore from the Aaron Halo or mined from microTech's surface is processed here and sold to manufacturers across the system. The margins are thin, the competition is fierce, and the occasional "accounting error" has been known to determine the fate of small trading concerns.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Docking Ring" category="settlement" image="/images/port-tressler/docking-ring.webp" imageAlt="Ships docked along the station's docking ring">

The Docking Ring is where Port Tressler earns its keep. At any given time, up to two hundred ships of various sizes and conditions are moored to the station's exterior, connected by pressurized umbilicals that supply power, life support, and data connections. The ring is a continuous loop of docking ports, arranged in a spiral that allows ships to be loaded and unloaded with mechanical efficiency.

The ring is segregated by ship class: small personal vessels on the inner ring, mid-sized freighters on the middle, and the occasional capital ship on the outer berths. The segregation is enforced by docking fees and physical constraints — the inner ring is not rated for ships over a certain mass, and the outer ring is too expensive for anyone without corporate backing.

For the average pilot, the Docking Ring is a transient space — a place to park, refuel, and move on. But for the station's permanent residents, the ring is a community. Dock workers, mechanics, and cargo handlers form a closed society with its own hierarchy, its own slang, and its own way of looking at the ships that come and go.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Station Bar" category="hidden-find" image="/images/port-tressler/station-bar.webp" imageAlt="The dimly lit station bar">

Every station has one, and Port Tressler is no exception. The Station Bar is a small, dimly lit establishment tucked into a corner of the Main Concourse, accessible only by a narrow corridor that most travelers walk past without noticing. The bar has no name — it is simply "the bar" to those who know it exists.

The interior is a deliberate rejection of the station's corporate aesthetic. The lighting is warm and low, the furniture is mismatched and comfortable, and the walls are covered in a patchwork of stickers, graffiti, and commemorative plaques from ships that have come and gone. It feels like a place where real humans have spent real time, which, on Port Tressler, is a novelty.

The bartender is a woman named Mei who has been here for seven years and remembers the name of every regular who has passed through. She doesn't talk much, but she listens — and in a place where information is currency, that makes her one of the station's most valuable assets. If you need to know which cargo manifest is light on inspection, which refinery contractor is desperate for work, or which docking inspector can be persuaded to look the other way, Mei knows. Her rates are reasonable: a drink and a story.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="The Observation Lounge" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/port-tressler/observation-lounge.webp" imageAlt="The observation lounge with a panoramic view of microTech">

The Observation Lounge is Port Tressler's one concession to aesthetics. Located at the station's rotational axis, where the centrifugal force is weakest, the lounge is a large, circular room with transparent walls that offer an unobstructed view of microTech below. On a clear day, you can see the curvature of the planet, the ice caps glinting at the poles, and the aurora that dances above the magnetic poles.

The lounge is free to enter, which makes it one of the station's most democratic spaces. Cargo handlers sit next to corporate executives. Smugglers share tables with law enforcement. The view has a way of putting things in perspective — when you're looking down at an entire planet, your personal concerns tend to shrink.

The best time to visit is during a solar storm, when the ionized particles in Stanton's solar wind interact with microTech's magnetosphere to create light shows that put any laser display to shame. The lounge spends those hours packed to capacity, with people standing shoulder to shoulder, staring out at a sky on fire.

</AttractionEntry>

<TouristTip>

- **Documentation**: Port Tressler's traffic control is thorough. Have your manifests, licenses, and cargo declarations ready before you approach. Delays caused by incomplete paperwork are charged by the hour.
- **Currency**: microTech credits are the preferred currency, but UEC is accepted at most vendors. Exchange rates are fair.
- **Food**: The station's food options are functional, not memorable. Skip the concourse vendors and head to the Docking Ring, where the dock workers have established a small ecosystem of food stalls that cater to actual human tastes.
- **Accommodation**: Port Tressler has no hotels per se, but the station's habitation rings rent short-term quarters to transient pilots. Small, utilitarian, and surprisingly comfortable, with a view of microTech that justifies the premium pricing.
- **Work**: If you need a cargo run, check the information kiosks in the Main Concourse. If you need a more discreet arrangement, ask at the Station Bar.
- **Repairs**: The station's repair facilities are excellent but expensive. Get a quote from at least three mechanics on the Docking Ring before committing.

</TouristTip>
