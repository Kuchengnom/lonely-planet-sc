<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The First River" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/microtech/ships-on-lake.jpeg" imageAlt="Ships parked at the edge of the first river lake basin">

On a planet where exposed skin freezes in minutes, finding open water feels like a trick. The river is no trick. It cuts through a thawed valley in the northern hemisphere with an absolute indifference to the permafrost surrounding it on every side, and geologists in the game's lore have never produced a satisfying explanation for why it exists.

From **New Babbage**, point your ship to heading **205** and quantum toward **MT DataCenter E2Q-NSG-Y**. Cancel the jump when the remaining distance to the data centre reads about **900 km**. Below you, look for a dark strip where the white breaks — a thawed valley of wet rock, scrub, and open water. Descend slowly. The river is easy to miss at speed and impossible to find once you've passed it.

The lake basin at the river's widest point is the most photographed spot. Land on the bank and walk to the water's edge. It is, genuinely, one of the stranger moments Stanton has to offer.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Canyon River" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/microtech/canyon-river-aerial.jpeg" imageAlt="Aerial view of the canyon river winding through autumn forest">

The newer discovery is harder to reach than the first river, but it rewards the detour with canyon walls, a deeper colour palette, and an approach that feels like genuine exploration rather than a nav-point run.

Quantum to **MT DataCenter KH3-AAE-L**, then hold heading **027** and fly roughly **440 km** while maintaining altitude. Watch the terrain shift from uniform snowfield to something with structure — ridgelines, cuts, deeper shadow. When the canyon shape becomes clear from above, slow down and descend along its length. Two alternate fixes if you lose it: search the planet-facing midpoint between **OM-6** and **OM-3**, or start from **Dunboro** settlement and fly heading **265** for about **60 km**.

The canyon itself is wide enough to fly a small ship through at low speed. Do that before you land.

</AttractionEntry>

<ExplorerView author="Survey Pilot, New Babbage">
The trick on microTech is patience. You're going to fly right over what you're looking for at least once — the rivers are invisible from height and the storms don't respect your schedule. The moment you decide to turn back is usually the moment the weather clears. Stay low on the return leg and watch the terrain change beneath you. That is when you find the things that aren't on any guide.
</ExplorerView>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Frozen Lakes" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/microtech/ice-arch.jpeg" imageAlt="Ice arch formation rising from a frozen lake on MicroTech">

Scattered across the sub-polar latitudes, microTech's frozen lakes are a different kind of stillness from the rivers — no anomaly, no mystery, just flat white expanses carved by a planet doing exactly what physics requires of it. What makes them worth landing for are the formations the ice builds on top of itself over time: arches, columns, and abstract blocks of compressed blue-white that look like sculpture and have no practical explanation beyond weather.

The ice-arch formations are the most photographed features on the surface after the rivers. They appear most reliably in the shallower lake regions where wind sculpts the ice before it's buried by snow. You can walk them in an appropriate suit — the footing is uneven and the ice closer to the water's edge is unreliable, but the views are worth the caution.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Bioluminescent Caves" category="hidden-find" image="/images/microtech/bioluminescent-cave.jpeg" imageAlt="Bioluminescent cave ceiling covered in blue-cyan glowing organisms">

Ask any explorer who has stumbled into one of these and they will use the same word: otherworldly. Shallow cave systems across the surface host ceilings and walls dotted with blue-cyan bioluminescent growth — thousands of pinpoint lights in complete darkness that make the rock above you look like inverted starfield.

They are not on any official map, which is half the appeal. Finding one requires low-altitude flying across the tundra biome and watching the terrain for dark openings in cliff faces and hillsides. Enter slowly — the caves are not deep but the floor is uneven and the dark is absolute beyond your suit lights. This is one for pilots who fly without a particular destination in mind and end up somewhere they didn't expect.

<TouristTip>
  Approach any cave opening from the side before committing your ship. The entrance height can be deceptive from above, and some openings large enough to enter on foot are too narrow for a Pisces. Park outside and walk in.
</TouristTip>

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="New Babbage" category="settlement" image="/images/microtech/flying-over-mountains.jpeg" imageAlt="Small exploration ship flying over MicroTech's snowy mountain ranges toward New Babbage">

The only city on microTech is a marvel of corporate ambition and a small mercy after wilderness flying. The towers are too tall for a planet this cold, the plazas too clean to feel lived-in, and the whole place has the quality of a concept render that was built without anyone checking whether people would actually enjoy it. And yet, after a long survey flight with deteriorating weather and a near-empty fuel tank, the lights of **New Babbage** on the horizon are the most welcome sight on the planet.

The **Commons** shopping area at ground level is worth a slow walk — coffee, gear, and an architectural grandeur that borders on absurdist. If you visit between December and January, the enormous **Christmas Tree** that appears in the main plaza is one of the game's most earnest and unexpectedly human moments: a massive festive decoration on a corporate ice planet, erected for no apparent reason beyond the fact that someone at microTech thought it was a good idea.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={6} title="Surface Outposts" category="settlement" image="/images/microtech/surface-outpost.jpeg" imageAlt="Ruined surface outpost buried in snow among pine trees">

The ruined outposts scattered through the snow-pine forests are part crash site, part mystery, and entirely worth a detour if you are already flying the wilderness. The antenna arrays, structural frames, and scattered equipment suggest long-term habitation; most of them look like they were abandoned in a hurry and never returned to.

**Dunboro** is the most accessible starting point — reachable from **MT DataCenter KH3-AAE-L** on heading **265** for roughly **60 km** — and functions as a working outpost rather than a ruin, making it a useful waypoint between the canyon river and the return leg to New Babbage. Smaller unnamed crash sites appear throughout the tundra with no nav markers. Land next to the antenna towers and have a walk around.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={7} title="The Forest Biomes" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/microtech/canyon-forest.jpeg" imageAlt="Autumn forest biome on MicroTech with orange foliage and rocky outcrops">

At the latitudes where snow gives way to tundra, the landscape changes in a way that photographs struggle to convey. The trees are frost-edged pine, dense enough to crowd the sky, and the ground between them is covered in orange-red autumn undergrowth that makes the whole scene look as though it's caught mid-season — which, on a planet with microTech's orbital mechanics, it effectively always is.

The canyon river cuts through the centre of the richest forest biome on the planet, which is the secondary reason to fly that route slowly. This is where microTech stops looking like a hostile environment and starts looking like somewhere a person might actually want to be. If you have a small ship with good atmospheric handling, drop below the treeline and follow the forest contours south. The transition back to snowfield is abrupt enough that the exact line feels like a boundary someone drew.

</AttractionEntry>
