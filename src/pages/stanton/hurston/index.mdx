<AttractionEntry number={1} title="Lorville Workers District" category="settlement" image="/images/hurston/workers-district.jpeg" imageAlt="The gritty Workers District of Lorville, with the Central Tower looming in the background">

The heart of Lorville isn't the corporate headquarters — it's Leavsden Square, a cramped plaza in the Workers District where miners, haulers, and failed entrepreneurs trade stories and complaints. The buildings are cheap, pre-fabricated units stacked without care, and the whole place smells of recycled air and industrial waste. Yet this is where the city lives. M&V's Bar sits at the edge of the square, a dimly-lit dive where the beer is local and the music is worse. But the atmosphere is real. This is a place where conversations happen, where contracts are whispered over sticky tables, and where the bartender knows more about Hurston's underworld than the Hurston Security HQ.

The Central Tower looms over everything, a monument to corporate ego built on the backs of people who can't afford the view from the top. Walk from Leavsden to the strip mine boundary at night and listen to the machinery. It never stops. The sound becomes a kind of lullaby after a while — the soundtrack of a city that works itself to death.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="MacIntyre & Victor's Bar" category="hidden-find" image="/images/hurston/mv-bar.jpeg" imageAlt="Interior of the dimly-lit M&V's Bar">

Every city has a bar that should not be as good as it is. M&V's is Lorville's. It sits at the edge of the Workers District, tucked between a clothing outlet and a security checkpoint, an afterthought that became a destination. The decor is what happens when someone tries to recreate "industrial chic" on a budget that wouldn't cover the sound system. The drinks are local, which means cheap and strong, and the menu has never heard of a vegetable. But the people-watching is unmatched.

Come at shift change — roughly 6pm local — when miners fresh from the strip mines trade seats with haulers waiting for the next run to Everus Harbor. The conversations are loud, opinionated, and occasionally violent. The security guard at the door is ornamental. And the bartender? He has a memory for faces and a tolerance for stories that makes the place feel like a confessional. Just don't mention Hurston Dynamics unless you want to start an argument.

Anyone who claims to know the city will eventually mention Clovus Darneely. He operates out of the Reclamation & Disposal yard near the Workers District and has a line on contracts that Hurston Security wouldn't touch. Ask around. Someone will point you in the right direction, usually with a warning to watch your back.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Strip Mine Biomes" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/hurston/strip-mine.jpeg" imageAlt="Aerial view of the Hurston strip mines">

Hurston was a savannah once. Before Hurston Dynamics arrived, stripped it, and moved on to the next resource patch. What's left is a patchwork of devastation and strange, accidental beauty. The strip mine biomes are the most accessible — a landscape of exposed earth, industrial runoff, and machinery the size of buildings. At sunset, the sky goes the colour of rust and copper, and the pollution scatters the light into something that photographers call "the Hurston Glow." It is spectacular and deeply wrong.

The Strip Mine itself connects to the Workers District via the worker train line that runs past M&V's Bar. You can follow it on foot, though the security checkpoints are frequent and the guards are not known for their diplomacy. The mesa biomes are a different challenge — a maze of ship wreckage, industrial debris, and navigation hazards that make ground vehicle travel a test of patience. This is where the best salvage is, and where the most interesting photographs happen. Just don't expect to find soft ground.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="Maria Pure of Heart" category="practical" image="/images/hurston/maria-hospital.jpeg" imageAlt="The exterior of Maria Pure of Heart hospital in the Workers District">

Every city's hospital is a window into something. Maria Pure of Heart, in the Workers District, is a window into the cost of Hurston's economy. The waiting area is permanently full. The staff are overworked and underpaid. And the most common diagnosis is "respiratory distress from unregulated industrial exposure." Tourists don't usually end up here. But if you do, the experience is sobering in a way that Lorville's corporate propaganda never is.

It is named for someone who probably never existed, or existed in a way that made the branding possible. The irony is not lost on the nurses.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="Tammany & Sons" category="practical" image="/images/hurston/tammany-sons.jpeg" imageAlt="Interior of the gritty Tammany & Sons clothing and supply shop">

Lorville's most-stocked outfitter is a reminder that even in a company town, someone is making money on the side. Tammany and Sons sits in the Workers District and sells everything from basic armor and ship components to filter masks and the occasional item that probably fell off a transport. The prices are reasonable, the stock is unpredictable, and the owner has a particular skill for finding things that Hurston Dynamics would rather you didn't have.

If you are new to Lorville, this is where you come to buy your first filter mask. If you have been here a while, this is where you come when Hurston Security cracks down and you need something that isn't on the official inventory. Just don't ask too many questions about where the stock came from. No one at Tammany's answers questions they don't have to.

<TouristTip>
  Bring a filter mask. The air in Lorville is technically breathable but practically unpleasant. Gyson Ready-Up softshell helmets are available at Tammany & Sons if you forgot yours, or if the one you brought couldn't handle the particulate count.
</TouristTip>

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={6} title="The Central Business District" category="settlement" image="/images/hurston/central-tower.jpeg" imageAlt="The imposing Hurston Dynamics Central Tower overlooking Lorville">

The Central Business District is Hurston Dynamics' headquarters, and it looks like exactly what it is: a monument to corporate power built on the broken back of a planet. The tower dominates the skyline, visible from any point in the city, a constant reminder of who owns the air you are breathing. Inside, the flagship store sells Hurston Dynamics merchandise to people who already paid for it with their health and their land. The Transfers Commodity Exchange handles transactions that would make a less-corrupt system pause.

The Archibald Hurston Memorial Hall is open to visitors. It is, predictably, a shrine to the family that bought the planet. The irony is unacknowledged and absolute. Photograph it from the Workers District for maximum effect. The contrast says more than any description could.

</AttractionEntry>

<ExplorerView slot="explorer" author="Dockworker, Leavsden Square">
The sunsets are why you stay. Everyone says the same thing. The work is hard, the air is poison, and the company takes more than it gives. But at six o'clock, when the light comes through the pollution just right, the whole city turns the colour of a dying ember. That's when you remember why you came. That's when you remember what you lost.
</ExplorerView>

<AttractionEntry number={7} title="The Shanty Towns" category="hidden-find" image="/images/hurston/shanty-town.jpeg" imageAlt="Makeshift structures in the shanty towns beyond Lorville's city walls">

Beyond the six perimeter gates, where the no-fly-zone ends and the desert begins, the shanty towns are a reminder that Hurston's economy has losers as well as winners. Makeshift structures, built from whatever could be salvaged from the strip mines, house workers whose contracts expired, whose health failed, or who simply could not meet the productivity quotas that Hurston Dynamics demands.

The security presence is lighter here. So is the enforcement of Hurston's laws. The people who live in the shanty towns have a particular hospitality for outsiders, born from a shared experience of being on the wrong side of a line someone else drew. If you go, bring something to trade. Water is currency. Information is safer.

</AttractionEntry>
