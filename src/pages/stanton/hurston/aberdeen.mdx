## Vitals

- **Type**: Moon (Former Ecosystem / Barren)
- **Orbits**: Hurston (Stanton I)
- **Atmosphere**: Sulfur dioxide and sulfur oxide, trace sodium chloride. Yellow-tinted. Lethal without full environmental sealing.
- **Average Temperature**: 230°C (daytime surface). Cold is not a concern here.
- **Gravity**: 0.31 G
- **Day Length**: ~21 Standard Hours
- **Notable**: The only Hurston moon that once had thriving grasslands and trees. It does not anymore.

## Getting Here

Nobody comes to Aberdeen voluntarily. That's not rhetoric — it's logistics. The moon has no spaceport, no landing beacon, no tourism infrastructure. The single authorized facility on the surface manages its own arrivals through UEE criminal processing, and if you're on their guest list, you already know how you got here.

Pilots flying in under their own authority will find the approach uncomplicated and the welcome non-existent. Quantum jump to Hurston's outer Lagrangian and sublight from there — Aberdeen sits close enough to Hurston that the gas giant's gravity well keeps the orbital mechanics orderly. The difficulty isn't getting here. It's everything that happens after you land.

Surface conditions are hostile in the specific way that accountants understand: the temperature is high enough to damage unshielded equipment and lethal to unprotected humans, the visibility is reduced by permanent haze, and the atmosphere will infiltrate any compromised seal within minutes. This is not a moon that forgives maintenance shortcuts.

## The Landscape

Aberdeen's surface is what remains after optimism runs out. The moon is the color of a healing bruise — ochre and rust and sulfur yellow in horizontal bands that shift as the light changes, which isn't often, because the light never quite penetrates the haze properly.

The dead ecosystem is the first thing that strikes anyone who knows the history. Scattered across the eastern basin, preserved in the dry toxic air like museum pieces, are the brittle remnants of what Aberdeen was before Hurston Dynamics decided it was a useful place to test things. Root structures. Petrified stems. The occasional skeletal trunk, still standing because nothing has the energy to knock it over. Forensic botanists who've analyzed samples date the last growing things to roughly three generations ago. No one from Hurston Dynamics has publicly discussed what killed them.

The western hemisphere is dominated by the Klescher operations perimeter. Even from the air, the facility reads as purpose-built for a population that would rather be elsewhere — a low, sprawling complex of sealed corridors and processing bays surrounded by layers of security infrastructure that looks more like environmental protection than containment. At 230°C, the distinction is academic.

### 1. The Klescher Rehabilitation Facility

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="Klescher Rehabilitation Facility" category="hidden-find" image="/images/aberdeen/klescher-facility.webp" imageAlt="The low grey bulk of the Klescher Rehabilitation Facility under Aberdeen's yellow sky">

The Klescher Rehabilitation Facility is the oldest and most efficient privatised corrections operation in the Stanton system, and the fact that those two qualities coexist should tell you everything you need to know about it. Klescher manages the facility under UEE contract. The UEE maintains that it does not operate prisons. The lawyers on both sides are paid well enough to find this arrangement satisfying.

What the facility actually does: it takes people who have broken the law with sufficient enthusiasm to earn a CrimeStat designation, transports them to a moon at 230°C with a lethal atmosphere, and gives them mining equipment. In return, inmates receive room, board, and a declining balance of time remaining. The mining operation is profitable. Klescher does not publish its financials.

For the visitor who arrives here against their will, the experience is clarifying. The processing corridors are functional and featureless. The dormitory allocation is done without ceremony. The work schedule is not posted as a request. And the horizon — visible through reinforced viewing panels that are more thoughtful than anything else about the facility — is an unbroken line of yellow haze, a reminder that the closest alternative to this room is an unbreathable atmosphere at temperatures that would cook you in your suit.

Aberdeen's heat isn't just environmental. It's the business model.

</AttractionEntry>

### 2. The Dead Grasslands

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Dead Grasslands" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/aberdeen/dead-grasslands.webp" imageAlt="Petrified tree stumps and desiccated root systems in the toxic dust of Aberdeen's eastern basin">

Aberdeen was once the only moon in the Hurston system with a functioning ecosystem. Not a rich one — a moon ecosystem, modest by any measure — but real. Grasses, scattered trees, probably insects. Something lived here. The soil records show root systems, seed dispersal patterns, the biological logic of a landscape that believed in itself.

The official line from Hurston Dynamics on Aberdeen's ecosystem collapse is a short sentence in a longer document: "geological and atmospheric shifts resulted in the cessation of viable plant life." The unofficial line, whispered between those who've studied the surface chemistry, is that the patterns of destruction are too specific, too directional, too concentrated near the former testing sites to be natural.

What remains sits in the eastern basin: petrified trunks that rise two meters from the ochre dust, their wood preserved by the dry toxic air. Root systems exposed by years of erosion, branching through the soil like frozen lightning. And fine-ground mineral dust that, in the right analysis, shows traces of compounds that don't occur naturally in volcanic geology but do appear in the accelerant profiles of high-temperature munitions.

Aberdeen's trees are a crime scene. No one has been charged.

</AttractionEntry>

### 3. The Aberdeen Gold

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Aberdeen Gold" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/aberdeen/sulfur-sky.webp" imageAlt="Aberdeen's sulfur-yellow sky filtering the distant sun into warm amber tones">

Photographers who've spent time on Aberdeen — voluntarily or otherwise — tend to mention the light. The sulfur dioxide in the atmosphere doesn't just make the sky yellow. It filters the sun through layers of chemical haze in a way that produces, in the two hours around local noon, a quality of light that has no equivalent in the cleaner atmospheres of more hospitable worlds.

The effect is called the Aberdeen Gold by the small community of industrial photographers who collect extreme-environment images. It's the color that light turns when it's been working too hard to get through: amber that shades to copper, with a faint greenish cast at the horizon where the sulfur is densest. It makes everything look both ancient and recent at once — the ruins of the dead ecosystem glow warmly under it, which is the most troubling thing about it.

From the Klescher facility's reinforced viewing ports, the Gold is available on a daily schedule. Guests of the facility have reported it as the thing they miss most once released. The facility management has, presumably, not thought too hard about what it means that their inmates miss the view.

</AttractionEntry>

### 4. The Geology of Deterrence

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Geology of Deterrence" category="practical" image="/images/aberdeen/heat-shimmer.webp" imageAlt="Heat shimmer rising from Aberdeen's surface, distorting the landscape">

There is a genre of prison architecture that relies on remote location as its primary containment strategy. Aberdeen is its masterpiece. Klescher didn't build the walls that keep people in — the moon did that. The 230°C surface temperature in direct sunlight. The atmosphere that will kill an unprotected human in minutes. The nearest habitable environment several hundred kilometers of vacuum away.

Walking the perimeter of the Klescher facility without authorization is possible. The security perimeter is not impermeable. But between the facility's edge and anything useful, there is only Aberdeen — and Aberdeen has been a better guard than any fence.

Geologically, the moon is consistent with other Hurston satellites: low gravity, resource-bearing regolith, the kind of subsurface mineral distribution that made it interesting to a mining corporation. What makes Aberdeen unique is that the same conditions that make mining profitable also make escape implausible. The heat that cooks ore out of rock will cook an unshielded human just as effectively. Klescher didn't design this. They just noticed it.

</AttractionEntry>

### 5. The One Way Out

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="The One Way Out" category="hidden-find" image="/images/aberdeen/security-terminal.webp" imageAlt="A security terminal in the facility's outer processing corridor">

It would be irresponsible to confirm that there is an escape route from the Klescher Rehabilitation Facility on Aberdeen. It would also be dishonest to deny it.

What can be said with some confidence is this: the facility's security infrastructure has known points of interface. CrimeStat designation, which is the mechanism by which the UEE flags individuals for Klescher processing, is a data entry in a networked system. Networked systems have terminals. Terminals can, in theory, be accessed. And terminals that can be accessed can sometimes be persuaded, with the right cryptographic tools, to revise entries.

Whether this theoretical sequence of events has been practically demonstrated is a matter of public record — prisoners have returned from Aberdeen before, with cleared designations, asking everyone not to discuss how. What they needed: time, patience, a Cryptokey of sufficient specification, and access to the right part of the facility at the right time.

What they also needed: a ship waiting. Aberdeen offers nothing once you're outside the Klescher perimeter. The heat, the atmosphere, the distance from any orbital option — these remain. The exit from prison is not the exit from Aberdeen.

Plan accordingly.

</AttractionEntry>

## Getting Around

There is no around to get. The Klescher Rehabilitation Facility is the only structure on Aberdeen's surface, and it is not organized around the needs of the independent traveler. The moon has no ground vehicle rental, no shuttle service, and no landing pad infrastructure beyond Klescher's own operations bay.

Pilots arriving under their own power should land as close to the facility as security allows and treat their ship as the only safe environment on the surface. Aberdeen's heat will damage unshielded external equipment over an extended stay — engine cooling systems in particular need monitoring, as the ambient temperature is high enough to reduce their efficiency margin.

Surface EVA should be kept to the minimum necessary. The atmosphere's sulfur compounds infiltrate suit seals at an accelerated rate compared to cleaner environments. Budget your time outside accordingly, and carry twice the filtration replacements you think you'll need.

For those departing the facility through official channels: Klescher operates its own transport service. Departure is on their schedule, not yours.

<TouristTip number={6}>

- **Temperature First**: Aberdeen's surface temperature will destroy unshielded equipment and kill unprotected humans within minutes. This is not a dramatic exaggeration. It is the operating manual. Read it before you land.
- **Suit Chemistry**: Standard EVA suits handle vacuum and cold well. They are not optimized for 230°C acidic atmospheres. Before visiting Aberdeen, verify your suit's thermal rating and acid resistance certification. If it doesn't have one, don't come.
- **Filtration Maintenance**: The sulfur dioxide atmosphere accelerates chemical degradation in standard scrubbers. Carry spares. Double your expected filtration consumption for an Aberdeen environment.
- **The Light**: If you are here voluntarily and you have a camera, shoot during the two hours around local noon. The Aberdeen Gold is real and it doesn't appear in documentation anywhere near often enough. Use it.
- **Exit Strategy**: You need one before you land. Aberdeen has no emergency services, no rescue infrastructure, and no interest in your wellbeing. Your ship is your life support. Keep it running and keep it accessible.
- **On the Subject of the Facility**: The Klescher Rehabilitation Facility does not offer tours, does not accept visitors, and does not respond to press inquiries. If you are reading this guide from inside it, we hope the information above is useful. We make no representations about its accuracy.

</TouristTip>
