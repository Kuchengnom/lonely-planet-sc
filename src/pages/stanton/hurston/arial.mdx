## Vitals

- **Type**: Moon (Barren / Toxic Atmosphere)
- **Orbits**: Hurston (Stanton I)
- **Atmosphere**: Primarily nitrogen with trace methane and hydrogen. Fully toxic to humans. No useful oxygen content.
- **Average Temperature**: Considerably above the boiling point. The surface shimmers. Do not mistake the shimmer for water.
- **Gravity**: 0.33 G
- **Day Length**: ~18 Standard Hours
- **Named After**: Arial Hurston, 3rd CEO of Hurston Dynamics — architect of the Life/Labor employee contract

## Getting Here

Arial sits in close orbit around Hurston, which means the gas giant's industrial haze gives way to Arial's subtler, more personal kind of hostility as you approach. Quantum jump from any of Hurston's Lagrangian stations and sublight in. The approach is unremarkable. Arial doesn't announce itself.

The two Hurston Dynamics Mining Stations — HDMS-Lathan and HDMS-Bezdek — serve as the moon's de facto landing zones. Neither was built with tourism in mind, which is accurate to the spirit of the moon. Lathan operates as a trade hub; Bezdek focuses on extraction. Both offer fuel and basic maintenance. Neither offers anything resembling warmth, architectural or otherwise.

There is no civilian spaceport. There is no commercial shuttle service. There is Hurston Dynamics, and there is the moon, and both of them are indifferent to your presence.

## The Landscape

Arial's surface is the colour of pale bone under a sky that refuses to commit to any particular hue. The nitrogen atmosphere catches the light from Hurston's distant sun and distributes it evenly, eliminating shadows, flattening perspective, reducing the entire moonscape to a series of planes in slightly different shades of nothing. It would be alienating if it were more dramatic. Instead it's alienating in the way that bureaucracy is alienating — through the complete absence of anything to push against.

The heat shimmer is the defining visual feature. Everything at ground level appears to float. Rocks look detached from the surface. The HDMS stations, seen from a distance, seem to hover above their own foundations. It is the kind of visual unreality that the brain refuses to fully process, insisting that water must be somewhere nearby. There is no water. There has never been water on Arial in any meaningful quantity. The shimmer is purely thermal — the atmosphere warping itself in the heat — and it is relentless from sunrise to sunset.

The terrain itself is undramatic: broad, gently rolling plains of desiccated regolith, occasional boulder fields, and the distant suggestion of ridge lines that never quite resolve into anything interesting. Arial is a moon without features, which is either peaceful or oppressive depending on your contract status.

### 1. HDMS-Lathan

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="HDMS-Lathan" category="settlement" image="/images/arial/hdms-lathan.webp" imageAlt="The Hurston Dynamics Mining Station Lathan on Arial's featureless plain">

HDMS-Lathan is the nearest thing Arial has to a town, which tells you a great deal about Arial. The station serves as the moon's primary trade hub — minerals extracted from the surface are processed here, logged, and dispatched to Hurston orbit and beyond. It also supplies the workers at Bezdek. In the corporate hierarchy, Lathan is the office. Bezdek is the quarry.

The station interior is functional to the point of philosophy. Every surface serves a purpose and no surface serves more than one. The lighting is adequate. The corridors are pressurised. The commissary provides nutrition, if not food. Workers rotate through on schedules set by the company, depart when their contracts permit, and are replaced by the next intake. The whole operation continues without acknowledgement of the individual, which Arial Hurston would have recognised immediately as the system working correctly.

The fuel and maintenance services at Lathan are reliable — Hurston Dynamics keeps its equipment operational, whatever else it does. For the independent traveller, this makes Lathan a useful stop on the inner system circuit, if not a destination. Come for the fuel. Leave before the atmosphere seeps into your thinking.

</AttractionEntry>

### 2. HDMS-Bezdek

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="HDMS-Bezdek" category="settlement" image="/images/arial/hdms-bezdek.webp" imageAlt="HDMS-Bezdek extraction facility against Arial's pale horizon">

Bezdek is where the actual work happens. The extraction station sits further into the moon's interior, positioned above the mineral-bearing geology that makes Arial worth operating on at all. The facility is smaller than Lathan and more purposeful — a place built around a task rather than around the people doing it.

The workers at Bezdek are, almost without exception, on Life/Labor agreements. The contract's terms are what Arial Hurston designed them to be: years-long, fixed-rate, with cancellation fees that exceed the earnings of the contract period. Workers arrive to pay off debts, to avoid Klescher, or because someone in Lorville told them the job came with housing and healthcare. All three things are technically true and practically inadequate.

What the Life/Labor contract does not say is that "housing" means a bunk assignment in Bezdek's dormitory module and "healthcare" means access to a certified medpen dispenser and a quarterly visit from a Hurston-contracted physician. It does not need to say this. The lawyers reviewed the language. The language is compliant.

The mineral extraction at Bezdek is efficient. Hurston Dynamics' equipment is well-maintained. The yields are documented precisely. Everything works as designed, which is the most honest thing about the place.

</AttractionEntry>

### 3. The Shimmer Plains

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Shimmer Plains" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/arial/shimmer-plains.webp" imageAlt="Arial's surface shimmering in the extreme heat, rocks appearing to float above the ground">

There is one genuinely beautiful thing about Arial, and it is entirely accidental. The moon's extreme surface temperature creates a thermal refraction effect of such consistency and scale that the entire landscape appears to levitate. Rocks hover. Ridge lines float. The HDMS stations, viewed from any distance, seem to drift above the surface like ships in a calm sea.

The effect is strongest in the two hours before and after local noon, when the temperature gradient between the surface and the lower atmosphere is most extreme. During this window, the shimmer turns the entire moon into a photographic subject — there is real aesthetic value in the way Arial deceives the eye, the way everything that is fixed and solid appears momentarily free.

Photographers who've documented Arial describe the shimmer as "the only honest lie the moon tells." Everything else about Arial is exactly what it appears to be. The shimmer at least offers the possibility of something else, even if that something else is an optical effect with a thermodynamic explanation.

For obvious reasons, surface EVA during peak shimmer hours is also peak temperature hours. Plan accordingly.

</AttractionEntry>

### 4. The Life/Labor Legacy

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Naming" category="hidden-find" image="/images/arial/arial-orbit.webp" imageAlt="Arial seen from orbit, its pale surface shimmering under Hurston's distant sun">

Hurston Dynamics named their moons after the family. Arial Hurston was the third CEO, and by most accounts the most architecturally significant — not for anything she built, but for the contract she designed in the early 26th century.

The Life/Labor agreement was, on its surface, an offer of stability. Guaranteed employment. Fixed rates. Years of income security. What it actually was, as critics and labour historians have noted at length, was a mechanism for eliminating exit. Workers who wanted to leave before their contract term ended paid cancellation fees that routinely exceeded the total value of their remaining earnings. Workers who tried to leave without paying were in breach of a legal agreement. The UEE reviewed the contract and found it compliant. The lawyers on both sides were paid well enough to find this satisfying.

What the historical record also shows — leaked through the Terra Gazette in the early 29th century — is that the company maintained meticulous internal documentation of minor infractions. Bathroom breaks. Unauthorised conversations. Brief production pauses. These records served as the basis for "off-booking" — termination for cause, which voided the healthcare and housing provisions without triggering the cancellation fee obligations. The contract that trapped workers going in contained a mechanism for removing them at cost when they became inconvenient.

The moon named after the woman who designed this system is a nitrogen-atmosphere hellscape where two Hurston Dynamics mining stations operate on contracts that Hurston Dynamics writes. The resonance is, presumably, unintentional.

</AttractionEntry>

### 5. Operating in the Shimmer

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="Operating in the Heat" category="practical" image="/images/arial/surface-approach.webp" imageAlt="A ship approaching an HDMS landing pad through Arial's heat-distorted atmosphere">

Arial's practical challenges are less dramatic than Aberdeen's but more insidious. The temperature is high enough to cause equipment stress without being the immediate kill condition that Aberdeen offers — Arial will damage your ship and degrade your suit over time rather than destroying both immediately. This creates a false sense of margin that experienced pilots warn against.

The atmosphere is the subtler threat. Nitrogen is not toxic in the way that Aberdeen's sulfur compounds are — it does not attack seals with the same aggression. What it does is displace oxygen, completely and without smell or warning. A compromised suit on Arial does not announce itself with irritation or nausea. It announces itself with loss of consciousness. Suit integrity checks are not optional on this moon.

The shimmer is also a navigation hazard. The thermal refraction that makes the plains beautiful makes accurate distance judgment extremely unreliable on approach. Pilots who've landed at Bezdek without prior experience report that the landing pad appears further than it is until it suddenly isn't. Use instruments. Do not trust your eyes on Arial. This advice extends metaphorically to the contract terms, but pilots usually learn that part later.

</AttractionEntry>

## Getting Around

Arial's two HDMS stations are roughly equidistant from each other across the moon's northern mineral belt, connected by a Hurston Dynamics internal transport service that is not available to non-contracted visitors. Independent travel between them requires a ship, which is also the only reliable way to reach surface locations outside the immediate station perimeters.

Surface rovers function adequately but require close temperature monitoring — Arial's ambient heat will stress standard cooling systems on extended ground operations. For short-range exploration near the stations, they're serviceable. For anything further, fly.

There is no public transport, no shuttle hire, and no ground service infrastructure outside the HDMS facilities. Hurston Dynamics provides services to Hurston Dynamics. Everybody else provides for themselves.

<TouristTip number={6}>

- **Suit Integrity is Everything**: Arial's atmosphere kills without warning. Oxygen displacement from a nitrogen atmosphere presents no sensory symptoms before incapacitation. Check your suit before EVA. Check it again. Do not rely on a single seal point — verify the full system.
- **The Shimmer Lies**: Thermal refraction makes distance judgment unreliable on the surface and during low-altitude approaches. Use your instruments for everything. The shimmer is beautiful. It is also trying to mislead you.
- **Equipment Temperature**: Arial's heat is chronic rather than acute. Your ship and suit will handle it — but monitor cooling systems on extended stays. The ambient temperature is high enough to reduce cooling margins significantly. Extended surface operations require active thermal management.
- **HDMS Services**: Fuel and basic maintenance are available at both stations. Provisions are available at Lathan. Do not expect hospitality — both facilities are operated as industrial installations and you are visiting, not welcomed.
- **The Contracts**: If someone at HDMS-Lathan offers you stable employment on favourable terms, read the cancellation clause before signing. Then read it again. Then consult a lawyer who is not employed by Hurston Dynamics.
- **The View**: The shimmer at noon, with Hurston filling a third of the sky behind it, is genuinely worth stopping for. Set a viewport reminder. Just don't go outside to look at it.

</TouristTip>
