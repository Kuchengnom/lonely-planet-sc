<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Caldera Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/cellin/caldera-fields.webp" imageAlt="A vast volcanic caldera with acid lake">

The hundred-plus calderas on Cellin are not, as you might expect, identical. Each has its own personality, its own chemistry, its own way of reminding visitors that they are standing in the throat of a planet that once coughed up a mountain.

The **Grand Caldera** is the largest — a depression thirty kilometers across, its floor a mosaic of dried lava flows and mineral deposits that shift color depending on the angle of Crusader's light. At dawn, it looks like a lake of molten copper. At midday, a bowl of dried blood. At dusk, a shadow so deep it seems to drink the light.

The **Acid Lakes** are exactly what they sound like: pools of sulfuric and hydrochloric acid, saturated with metal salts that render them in shades no natural body of water should achieve. Turquoise, jade, amber, and a color that can only be described as "aggressive violet." The acid is concentrated enough to dissolve standard EVA suit seals in hours, so viewing is strictly from the rim. The view, however, is worth the distance.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="Security Post Kareah" category="practical" image="/images/cellin/security-post-kareah.webp" imageAlt="The orbital Security Post Kareah above Cellin">

Security Post Kareah is not, strictly speaking, on Cellin. It orbits the moon, a converted monitoring station that Crusader Industries turned into a security checkpoint after one too many "incidents" in the local shipping lanes. The station's official purpose is "regional security coordination." Its actual purpose is to make sure nobody gets into — or out of — Cellin's volcanic fields without filing the appropriate paperwork.

For the average traveler, Kareah is an inconvenience. For the smuggler, it's an obstacle. For the bounty hunter, it's a potential payday. The station processes hundreds of ships per day, and its scanners are thorough enough to detect contraband hidden in engine coolant reservoirs, fake bulkhead compartments, and that "totally legitimate" cargo of mining equipment that weighs three times what it should.

The station commander maintains a strict "no bribes" policy, which everyone politely pretends to believe. The real currency at Kareah is information — who is moving what, where, and for whom. Pilots with useful intelligence sometimes find their inspections remarkably brief.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Lava Tubes" category="hidden-find" image="/images/cellin/lava-tubes.webp" imageAlt="Interior of a massive lava tube on Cellin">

When Cellin's volcanoes were active, they didn't just pour lava across the surface — they channeled it through underground tunnels that stretched for kilometers. Now, with the volcanic activity reduced to simmering, these tubes are hollow. And they are magnificent.

The largest tube system, the **Klescher Network** (named after the security corporation that briefly used them for unauthorized detention facilities), runs for over forty kilometers beneath the northern volcanic field. The tubes range in diameter from barely-ship-width to cavernous enough to park a Freelancer inside. The walls are lined with volcanic glass that catches even weak light and throws it back in fractured rainbows.

The tubes are structurally stable — mostly. But "mostly" is doing a lot of work there. The ground still trembles occasionally as magma shifts below, and sections of the network have collapsed without warning. The local wisdom is: if the walls start singing (a high-pitched harmonic vibration that precedes larger tremors), you have about sixty seconds to find an exit.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Glass Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/cellin/glass-fields.webp" imageAlt="The obsidian glass fields of Cellin reflecting light">

Where lava meets air and cools rapidly, it forms obsidian — volcanic glass, sharp as a blade and black as space. On Cellin, ancient lava flows created fields of obsidian so extensive they cover thousands of square kilometers. The result is a landscape that looks like a mirror shattered across a desert.

The Glass Fields are simultaneously one of the most beautiful and most dangerous places in the Stanton system. The obsidian reflects Crusader's light during the day and the stars at night, creating the illusion that you're standing on a solid mirror. But the glass is razor-sharp, and a fall here means lacerations that standard medpens can't fully address. EVA suits take damage just from brushing against the formations.

The truly remarkable feature is the **Singing Glass** — sections where the obsidian has formed into thin sheets that vibrate in the wind, producing harmonic tones that sound almost musical. The effect is unsettling in a way that is hard to describe: a vast, dark glass plain, humming to itself under an alien sky.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="Geothermal Vents" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/cellin/geothermal-vents.webp" imageAlt="Steam rising from geothermal vents on Cellin's surface">

Cellin's geothermal activity isn't entirely dormant — it's just resting. Vent fields dotted across the moon's surface release steam, sulfur dioxide, and occasional superheated water that has flash-boiled more than one overcurious rover. The vents are unpredictable, opening and closing on schedules that defy Coriolis prediction models.

The steam from these vents creates localized microclimates — pockets of warmth and humidity in an otherwise hostile environment. In these pockets, extremophile bacteria have established colonies that represent some of the most resilient life in the UEE. Scientists from microTech have been trying to study them for years, but the bacteria seem singularly uninterested in cooperating.

For travelers, the vents offer a practical advantage: free heat and water (after purification). Several enterprising pilots have established temporary shelters near reliable vent fields, using the geothermal energy to maintain life support without tapping their ship's power reserves.

</AttractionEntry>

## Getting Around

There is no formal transport network on the surface. Most exploration is done by rover or light ship. The volcanic fields near the equator are relatively flat ("relatively" being the operative word), but the caldera rims require careful navigation — the obsidian is sharp enough to damage landing gear, and the acid lakes don't discriminate between unintended and intentional contact.

Security Post Kareah functions as the de facto spaceport. From there, you can arrange shuttle service to the surface (expensive and unreliable), rent a rover (functional but elderly), or hire a local pilot who knows the landing zones that aren't on any map (expensive, unreliable, and possibly illegal, but effective).

<TouristTip>

- **Sulfur Filtration**: Cellin's atmosphere is thin but toxic. Standard EVA suits need enhanced sulfur filtration, which most don't come with. Check your scrubbers before departure.
- **Thermal Regulation**: The temperature swings between volcanic and non-volcanic areas are extreme. Your suit's heating/cooling system will work overtime. Plan accordingly.
- **Ground Stability**: That flat-looking patch of ground might be a lava tube roof that is thinner than your hull plating. When in doubt, avoid. When not in doubt, still avoid.
- **Crack Detection**: Volcanic glass is razor-sharp and invisible in low light. Carry extra suit patches. Lots of them.
- **Emergency Shelter**: The geothermal vents create warm zones. If you're stranded, find a vent field and stay near the warmth. The bacteria won't bother you (probably), and the steam might keep you alive until rescue.

</TouristTip>
