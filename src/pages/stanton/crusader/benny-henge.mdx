There is no marker on your map for Benny Henge. There is no quantum travel point, no official listing, no landing zone designation. What there is: an anonymous artist's life work, completed without him, drifting in the dark side of Yela's asteroid belt on a small rock with a gravity pad and two transmitter towers and a circle of stolen vending machines arranged in the style of one of Earth's oldest monuments.

The artist was killed during the final phase of construction. One of the machines fell on him. The monument was finished anyway — by whom, and how, the record doesn't say. The machines remained. The transmitters went on broadcasting. And somewhere in the Yela belt, on an asteroid that doesn't appear on any official chart, the Big Benny's jingle plays on a loop into the void, which is exactly what the artist intended and also, in a way he couldn't have planned, the most honest ending the piece could have had.

His stated message, recovered from datapads scattered at locations across the Crusader system: *"This is how the story of Humanity will be told. This corrupt consumerism will be the monument that we leave behind."*

He left behind a monument made of fast food machines. And also himself.

## Getting There

Benny Henge has no quantum marker. It sits in the umbra of Yela — the moon's permanent shadow, on the side facing away from Crusader's sun — which means the asteroid that hosts it receives no natural light and appears on no navigation database. Finding it is the first test of whether you deserve to see it.

**The approach:**

1. Quantum to **Yela OM-1** (North Pole orbital marker). This puts you in the right orbital band.
2. Drop to sublight and begin flying into the asteroid belt on the dark side of Yela.
3. Listen. Tune your radio receiver to scan open frequencies. At range, you will pick up a signal — the Big Benny's advertising jingle, on loop, broadcast from the machines themselves.
4. Follow the signal. The volume increases as you close distance. When you begin to see the first flashing beacons attached to the outer machines, you're close.

**The trail system:**

The machines don't just mark the destination — they form a navigational chain leading to it. Each machine has two features on its back panel:

- **An arrow**, painted or scratched, pointing toward the next machine in the sequence
- **A temperature in Kelvin**, a naturally significant value — used as a heat/cold indicator, warmer numbers pointing toward the henge, cooler numbers marking dead ends

Follow the arrows. The chain leads inward through the belt to the central asteroid. Below the two transmitter towers is a landing pad with functioning artificial gravity — land there. The henge is a short EVA from the pad.

<TouristTip>
  Location shifts between game patches — coordinates documented by the community become unreliable after updates. The beacon-and-jingle method is patch-agnostic: it works regardless of where the asteroid is in the belt. If you find a community guide with specific coordinates, treat them as a starting search zone, not a guaranteed destination.
</TouristTip>

## What You'll Find

The henge itself is a circle of Big Benny's brand vending machines, upright, arranged in the proportions of the Stonehenge monument from Earth. Each machine still has its branding intact — the red and white colour scheme, the logo, the product display panels showing items that have not been available for purchase since the machines were reported stolen from locations across the Crusader system.

The artificial gravity on the asteroid keeps everything grounded. The transmitter towers above serve dual purpose: they anchor the beacon signal and, from the right angle, frame the henge against the starfield in a way that no photograph adequately captures and every visitor attempts to capture anyway.

The site is quiet. The jingle is not — it plays continuously from the machines, which is either meditative or maddening depending on how long you've been travelling to get here. The effect of standing in a Stonehenge circle made of fast food dispensers, in the dark side of an asteroid belt, with a corporate jingle playing softly in your helmet speakers and Crusader's gas bands glowing in the middle distance, is genuinely difficult to describe to someone who hasn't experienced it. Bring someone to share it with. The artist apparently worked alone.

## The Artist

No name has been formally attached to the creator of Benny Henge. The community that has documented the site refers to him by his work. What the datapads left across the Crusader system establish: he lived at GrimHEX, he felt strongly about what commercial culture was doing to human expression, and he spent significant time and risk stealing Big Benny's machines from locations around the system and transporting them, one by one, to an unmarked asteroid in the Yela belt.

The machines are heavy. Moving them into position required EVA work on a spinning asteroid without a safety net. During the final phase of installation, one of the machines came loose. He did not survive the impact.

The transmitters kept broadcasting. The jingle kept playing. Other hands — or perhaps the machines had already been positioned and the transmitters pre-programmed — completed what he started. The piece exists. He does not.

The datapads contain one more line, beyond the quote about consumerism: *"If you're reading this, you found it. That's the point."*

## The Mission

The **Vending Machine Theft** is an untracked mission — it won't appear in your mission log, and completing it produces no formal reward. The mission begins at one of the scattered datapads at locations around the Crusader system and follows a trail of visual clues and additional datapads that document the theft, the artist's intent, and the henge's location methodology.

It is one of the few pieces of environmental storytelling in the Stanton system that does not involve a corporation, a bounty, or a delivery. It involves a person who believed something and built something to say it and died saying it. That's rarer than it should be.
