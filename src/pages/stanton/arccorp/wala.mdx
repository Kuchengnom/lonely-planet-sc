## Vitals

- **Type**: Moon (Barren / Tidally Deformed)
- **Orbits**: ArcCorp (Stanton III)
- **Shape**: Noticeably prolate — elongated rather than spherical, pulled long by ArcCorp's tidal forces on Wala's low-density body
- **Atmosphere**: Too thin to breathe. Pressure suits can extract usable oxygen directly from the environment.
- **Average Temperature**: Cold but not extreme — within suit tolerance without supplemental heating
- **Gravity**: 0.29 G
- **Day Length**: ~22 Standard Hours

## Getting Here

ArcCorp is visible from most of the inner system, which makes Wala findable by default — it's the small egg-shaped moon in the larger planet's orbital shadow. Quantum jump to any of ArcCorp's Lagrangian stations and sublight from there. The approach through ArcCorp's orbital traffic is the most complicated part; once clear of the planet's shipping lanes, Wala itself offers no resistance.

The four ArcCorp Mining Areas — 045, 048, 056, and 061 — are the moon's primary landing options, each equipped with basic services and maintained to the standard ArcCorp operational floor (functional, impersonal, reliably fuelled). Samson & Son's Salvage Center serves as an informal alternative for pilots who prefer not to deal with corporate check-in procedures. Both options have their specific advantages, depending on what you're there to do and what you're carrying when you arrive.

## The Landscape

Wala's defining characteristic is visible before you land: the shape. Low density makes the moon susceptible to ArcCorp's tidal forces in a way that denser bodies resist, and the result is a moon pulled slightly long in the direction of its parent planet. From a distance it looks like a ball someone sat on. From orbit, the effect is more striking — Wala is visibly, geometrically wrong in a way that no photograph quite captures, because photographs flatten what is actually a three-dimensional deformation.

The surface is barren in the specific way that geologically active bodies sometimes produce: not dead, exactly, but stripped down to fundamentals. No atmosphere worth breathing, no surface water, no organic chemistry. What remains is the mineral record — and that record is interesting. Across Wala's plains, in irregular clusters that follow subsurface fracture lines, geode-like formations break the surface. Some are modest, the size of a fist. Others are the size of a rover, their crystalline interiors visible where erosion or ArcCorp's equipment has opened them. The formations are not mined at scale — the extraction economics don't favour it — which means they persist, scattered across the terrain like abandoned gifts.

Between the geode clusters and the ArcCorp facilities, Wala's surface is undifferentiated grey-brown regolith under a sky too thin to scatter light properly. The stars are visible at noon. It is a moon that keeps nothing hidden except the things that are specifically trying to stay hidden.

### 1. The Geode Fields

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Geode Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/wala/geode-fields.webp" imageAlt="Crystal-lined geode formations breaking through Wala's grey surface, catching starlight">

The best thing about Wala is also the most overlooked. The geode formations that cluster along the moon's subsurface fracture lines are genuinely striking — pockets of crystalline mineral growth embedded in an otherwise featureless surface, their interiors refracting even weak light into spectral arrays that have no business being this far out from the sun.

The formations vary in size and composition. The smaller ones are quartz-adjacent: clear or slightly smoky, their crystal faces angled in the geometric precision that mineral growth produces when left alone long enough. The larger formations tend toward colour — amethyst-purple, citrine yellow, the occasional deep green that suggests trace metallic contamination in the growth medium. None of them are especially rare. All of them are beautiful, which is a distinction the mining industry regularly fails to honour.

ArcCorp's four surface operations extract from the mineral-bearing regolith rather than the geode formations directly. Whether this is a deliberate aesthetic decision or simply a reflection that the formations yield lower per-tonne value than the surrounding matrix is a question the company's extraction reports don't address. Either way, the geodes remain.

The best clusters are in the equatorial belt, between Mining Areas 048 and 056. The low gravity means a cautious explorer can traverse the field on foot in reasonable time. Bring a light source. The interiors come alive in a way that ambient starlight doesn't reveal.

</AttractionEntry>

### 2. The ArcCorp Mining Network

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="ArcCorp Mining Areas 045–061" category="practical" image="/images/wala/arccorp-mining-045.webp" imageAlt="An ArcCorp Mining Area outpost on Wala's barren plain, lights on against the dark sky">

Four outposts. One corporate owner. Minimal variation. ArcCorp Mining Areas 045, 048, 056, and 061 are the moon's official infrastructure — fuel, basic maintenance, commodity trading at rates that reflect ArcCorp's confidence in their position as the only game in the immediate area. Each facility extracts surface minerals for processing and shipment to Area18 and beyond.

The facilities are not destinations. They are service stations, and they perform that function without elaboration or warmth. The staff are contracted, the equipment is current-generation, and the commissary stocks things that are technically food. What the network does provide that matters is coverage: four evenly distributed outposts means that wherever you land on Wala's relatively compact surface, you're not far from fuel and a pressurised room.

Mining Area 061 is the largest of the four and functions as a modest trade hub — commodity buy and sell terminals, repair services one tier above basic, and the occasional contract notice posted on behalf of ArcCorp's extraction management. For pilots working the ArcCorp system circuit, Wala's mining network is a reliable logistics node that nobody talks about enthusiastically, which is exactly what a logistics node is supposed to be.

</AttractionEntry>

### 3. Samson & Son's Salvage Center

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="Samson & Son's Salvage Center" category="hidden-find" image="/images/wala/samson-salvage.webp" imageAlt="The cluttered yard of Samson & Son's Salvage Center, ship components stacked against the grey sky">

Samson & Son's is, by its own description, a family-run and family-operated salvage center proud of their quality service, reliability, and the best possible pricing in today's current markets. All of this is probably true. The salvage yard is well-stocked, the pricing is competitive, and the family in question has clearly been operating long enough to understand what the market wants.

The market, on Wala, wants a few specific things. One of them is salvage. Another is a non-ArcCorp landing option where the check-in process involves less documentation. The third is a delivery address that appears on certain mission contracts that wouldn't pass inspection at a corporate facility. Samson & Son's accommodates all three of these interests without advertising any of them directly.

The yard is not an Armistice Zone, which is relevant information delivered without editorial comment. It appears on the delivery list for contracts that are characterised, officially, as involving prohibited goods. Whether the Samson family knows this, profits from it, or simply rents the address without asking questions is a matter of operational inference. The quality of their salvage assessments is genuinely good. The rest is the customer's business.

</AttractionEntry>

### 4. Paradise Cove

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="Paradise Cove" category="hidden-find" image="/images/wala/paradise-cove.webp" imageAlt="The entrance to Paradise Cove, a low outpost building on Wala's surface with Nine Tails markings">

Paradise Cove has a name that suggests either someone's sense of humour or someone's optimism, and the current state of the facility suggests the former. The outpost sits on Wala's surface with the low profile of a building that would prefer not to be noticed, which is architecturally appropriate given what happens inside.

The Nine Tails logo visible in the interior office — the same insignia found at GrimHEX — is the most direct piece of information the facility offers about its affiliations. The Waste Removal missions that originate here are the second piece. The interior equipment configuration is the third, for those who make it inside with enough time to look around before the situation deteriorates.

Paradise Cove achieved broader notoriety as the anchor location for the Jumptown 2.0 and 2.1 dynamic events — periodic eruptions of organised chaos in which the facility's product becomes available for sale or theft depending on your relationship with the people currently holding it, and the Nine Tails dispatch ships to ensure the economics remain in their favour. The events draw participants from across the system, which means Wala's usually-quiet orbit becomes very crowded on a schedule that nobody publishes in advance.

Outside of event periods, Paradise Cove is quieter but not empty. Operational facilities tend to stay operational. The question of what they're operating is left as an exercise for the visitor.

</AttractionEntry>

### 5. The Processing Centers

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="ArcCorp Processing Centers 115 & 123" category="hidden-find" image="/images/wala/processing-center-123.webp" imageAlt="The reinforced entrance to ArcCorp Processing Center 123, turrets visible on the perimeter">

ArcCorp Processing Center 123 is an ArcCorp-owned underground bunker on Wala. So is Processing Center 115. Both are listed in ArcCorp's operational registry. Neither is accessible. Approaching either facility in a ship results in the perimeter turrets engaging, regardless of CrimeStat, regardless of transponder, regardless of any documentation you might have prepared.

This policy — defending ArcCorp property from ArcCorp's own customers — raises obvious questions that ArcCorp's communications department has not, to date, answered. What is being processed in facilities that shoot at ships carrying no outstanding warrants? Why does a corporation with a legitimate planetary monopoly need underground bunkers with automated defensive postures? What is the relationship between these facilities and the other underground operations on Wala that are more openly associated with criminal enterprise?

The answers are not available. The turrets are. This guide recommends observing both Processing Centers from an altitude and distance at which the turrets are a theoretical rather than practical consideration, and leaving the questions for someone with better resources and more patience than a travel writer.

The facilities are, technically, points of interest. They are also a reminder that "ArcCorp-owned" is not the same as "publicly accessible," and that the category of things ArcCorp processes on its moons is broader and less transparent than the annual report suggests.

</AttractionEntry>

## Getting Around

Wala is compact enough that ship-hopping between facilities is quick — the four mining areas and Samson's are within short sublight distances of each other, and the moon's low gravity makes surface approaches straightforward once you've cleared the orbital traffic from ArcCorp's shipping lanes.

Ground transport is viable near the outposts. The geode fields are walkable in low gravity with patience and directional sense — the terrain is flat enough to navigate but featureless enough that keeping a reference point in sight is advisable. The thin atmosphere means your suit's O₂ extraction system handles respiration without supplemental tanks, which removes one logistical constraint and replaces it with the obligation to monitor suit integrity carefully.

Avoid approaching the Processing Centers by ship. This advice is simpler and more emphatic than most navigational guidance, and it is the most important sentence in this section.

<TouristTip number={6}>

- **Suit O₂ Extraction**: Wala's atmosphere is too thin to breathe but rich enough that standard pressure suit systems can extract usable oxygen directly. You don't need supplemental tanks on this moon — but you do need an intact suit. Check seals before EVA. The O₂ extraction margin is not a substitute for suit integrity.
- **The Prolate View**: Wala's non-spherical shape is best appreciated from a specific angle: approach from ArcCorp's direction, from mid-altitude, and look toward the poles. The elongation is subtle from any single vantage but becomes geometrically obvious when you understand what you're looking for. It is a moon being pulled apart in slow motion. This is worth stopping to consider.
- **The Geodes**: Bring a hand light. The crystalline formations do not reveal themselves in ambient starlight the way they do with a direct source. The equatorial belt between Mining Areas 048 and 056 has the best clusters.
- **ArcCorp Pricing**: The four mining areas price fuel and provisions at ArcCorp's standard margin, which is to say: you can do better, but not on Wala. Factor this into your route economics before arrival rather than after.
- **Paradise Cove Timing**: If you arrive during a Jumptown event, Wala's orbit will be significantly more crowded than usual and significantly less safe. Know the difference between a quiet moon and a temporarily very loud one.
- **The Processing Centers**: Do not approach by ship. This is not a general safety recommendation. It is specific, practical, and based on the documented behaviour of the perimeter turrets.

</TouristTip>
