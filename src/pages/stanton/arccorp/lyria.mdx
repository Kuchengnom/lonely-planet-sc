<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Cryovolcano Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/lyria/cryovolcano-fields.webp" imageAlt="Active cryovolcanoes on Lyria's surface">

Lyria is home to over fifty active cryovolcanoes, with another two hundred in various stages of dormancy. Unlike traditional volcanoes, which announce their presence with smoke and fire, cryovolcanoes are subtle — until they're not. The eruption of a cryovolcano is not the dramatic explosion of popular imagination. It is a sudden, violent decompression that throws supercooled material kilometers into the air, where it flash-freezes and rains down as a storm of frozen shrapnel.

The **Aurora Caldera** is the most active — and most dangerous — of Lyria's cryovolcanoes. It erupts on average once every thirty-six hours, though "average" is doing a lot of work. The caldera is a depression three kilometers across, filled with a lake of cryomagma that shifts and cracks like a living thing. When it erupts, the plume of frozen gases and hydrocarbons rises ten kilometers before collapsing back down, coating the surrounding landscape in fresh deposits.

The **Purple Spires** are a related phenomenon — crystalline formations that grow around cryovolcanic vents. They are composed of frozen hydrocarbons and water ice, deposited layer by layer over centuries by the slow, continuous venting of gases. The result is a forest of purple and blue crystal formations that reach heights of fifty meters. They are also razor-sharp and structurally unstable. A friend of mine touched one once. She still has the scar.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="Research Outpost Alpha" category="settlement" image="/images/lyria/research-outpost.webp" imageAlt="ArcCorp's research outpost on Lyria">

ArcCorp maintains a small research station on Lyria's equatorial plain, ostensibly to study the cryovolcanic activity for "potential industrial applications." The station is a cluster of modular habitats connected by pressurized corridors, surrounded by a field of sensor equipment that looks like a mechanical garden.

The outpost is staffed by a rotating team of geologists, chemists, and engineers who have the mixed fortune of living on one of the most scientifically interesting and personally uncomfortable moons in the UEE. The station's amenities are minimal — hot water is a luxury, fresh food is nonexistent (everything is reconstituted), and privacy is a theoretical concept.

But the science is real. The researchers at Outpost Alpha have published some of the most significant papers on cryovolcanic mechanics in the last decade. They've also, according to persistent rumors, made some discoveries that ArcCorp has classified at the highest levels. What those discoveries are, no one will say. But the fact that ArcCorp has tripled the outpost's security budget in the last year is... interesting.

Visitors are not welcome, but they're not entirely unwelcome either. The scientists get lonely. If you show up with fresh fruit, real coffee, or news from the outside, you may find yourself invited in for a meal and a conversation that strays dangerously close to classified territory.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="The Purple Ice Cliffs" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/lyria/purple-cliffs.webp" imageAlt="The glowing purple ice cliffs of Lyria">

On Lyria's southern hemisphere, the moon's cryovolcanic activity has created a series of cliffs that defy conventional geology. They are not — despite appearances — carved by wind or water. They are formed by the slow, continuous deposition of frozen hydrocarbons from cryovolcanic vents, layer upon layer, over millions of years. The result is a wall of purple and blue ice that glows with an internal luminescence.

The cliffs are called the **Lilac Ramparts** by the researchers at Outpost Alpha, though no one is sure who coined the name. They stretch for over two hundred kilometers in a crescent formation, rising to heights of three hundred meters. In the right light — during the long Lyria twilight, when ArcCorp's reflected light bathes the moon in shades of copper and gold — the cliffs appear to be on fire with an inner light.

The ice of the Lilac Ramparts is not water-ice, or not entirely. It is a mixture of frozen water, methane, ammonia, and more exotic hydrocarbons that give it both its color and its fragility. The crystal structure is complex and beautiful, but a single miscalculation in temperature or pressure can cause entire sections to fracture. The sound of a section calving — a deep, resonant crack that echoes across the plain — is both magnificent and terrifying.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="Frozen Geyser Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/lyria/frozen-geysers.webp" imageAlt="Frozen hydrocarbon geysers on Lyria">

Lyria's cryovolcanoes are not the only evidence of the moon's geologic restlessness. Between the major cryovolcanic fields lie vast plains dotted with geysers that erupt not with water or steam but with frozen hydrocarbons and supercooled gases. These geysers are smaller than the cryovolcanoes but far more numerous — there are estimated to be over ten thousand active geysers across the moon's surface.

The geyser fields are surreal. Imagine a plain of black ice, stretching to the horizon, dotted with columns of frozen spray that catch the light like glass sculptures. Some geysers erupt continuously, creating frozen fountains that grow over centuries into elaborate spires. Others burst unpredictably, sending plumes of frozen particles thirty meters into the air before they collapse back down.

The largest geyser field, the **Frost Garden** (another Outpost Alpha coinage; the scientists have too much time on their hands), covers an area the size of a small continent. It is simultaneously one of the most beautiful and most dangerous places in the Stanton system. The geysers are unpredictable, the ground is unstable, and the frozen spray can damage unprotected equipment. And yet, pilots come here. Pilots always come here.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="The Subsurface Ocean" category="hidden-find" image="/images/lyria/subsurface-ocean.webp" imageAlt="Theorized subsurface ocean beneath Lyria's ice crust">

It is widely believed — though not yet proven conclusively — that Lyria harbors a subsurface ocean of liquid water and hydrocarbons beneath its frozen crust. The evidence is circumstantial but compelling: cryovolcanic eruptions that contain water in proportions inconsistent with surface ice, subsurface radar anomalies, and tidal flexing data that suggests a liquid layer.

If the subsurface ocean exists, it would be one of the most significant discoveries in the Stanton system. A liquid water ocean — even one mixed with hydrocarbons — is a prime candidate for the development of life. ArcCorp has funded three separate drilling missions, all of which failed due to equipment malfunction at depths exceeding two kilometers. The failures were officially attributed to "technical issues." The researchers at Outpost Alpha have their own theories.

Whatever is down there — water, hydrocarbons, or something else entirely — is currently inaccessible. But the question remains: what is Lyria hiding beneath its frozen crust? And why does ArcCorp seem so interested in finding out?

</AttractionEntry>

## Getting Around

There is no formal transport on Lyria. The moon's surface is not suitable for standard ground vehicles — the ice is too unpredictable, the hydrocarbon deposits too sticky, and the temperature too extreme for anything without specialized heating. Most exploration is done by light ship, hopping between sites, or by specialized rovers designed for cryogenic environments.

ArcCorp Research Outpost Alpha is the closest thing to a hub. From there, the researchers can provide — for a "materials donation" — basic supplies, emergency shelter, and the occasional meal that doesn't come from a packet. Do not rely on them. The outpost is not a hotel, and the scientists are not concierges.

<TouristTip>

- **Thermal Management**: Lyria's surface temperature will freeze standard EVA suit components in minutes. You need a suit rated for cryogenic environments. If your suit doesn't have a label that says "rated to -200°C," don't get out of the ship.
- **Cryomagma Awareness**: Cryovolcanic eruptions don't announce themselves with smoke. The ground may vibrate slightly, or the air pressure may drop, or you may notice nothing at all until the plume hits. Maintain distance from active calderas.
- **Ice Integrity**: The surface ice may conceal voids, caverns, or hydrocarbon deposits that are structurally weak. Ground-penetrating radar is not optional — it is survival equipment.
- **Emergency Protocol**: If stranded, find a cryovolcanic vent. The geothermal activity creates localized warm zones. You will still freeze, but it will take longer.
- **Geysers**: The frozen geyser fields are beautiful but dangerous. Do not approach within fifty meters of an active geyser. The frozen spray moves fast enough to damage suit visors and ship hulls.

</TouristTip>
