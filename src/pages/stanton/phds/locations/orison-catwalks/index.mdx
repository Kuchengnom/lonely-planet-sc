Orison is famous for its gas-giant vistas and the eternal twilight between platform and cloud. But there is a layer that most tourists never see: **the catwalks suspended in the thick cloud strata themselves**. These maintenance walkways, positioned at precise altitude bands, hover permanently in the swirling fog that surrounds Crusader. The result is an elevated perspective over the connected gas pumps that feels less like an industrial platform and more like walking on an invisible floor in a dreamscape.

On most mornings after a cloud reformation event (often dubbed a “reset” in community guides), the cloud layer density changes enough to reveal these walkways for a short window. During these minutes, the pathways are bathed in dramatic light refracting through the cloud particles. The floor seems to disappear beneath you. The distant platforms float in an ocean of white. It’s one of the most photographed backdrops in the Stanton system — and yet the bulk of notable shots in community albums were captured by pilots who stumbled upon these windows by accident.

## Getting There

These catwalks are located near the Transcom Science platform between Pump 3 and Pump 4. Access them from the Orison platforms (not from Area18, which is on ArcCorp). By convention across community threads, aim for roughly 75.2 m altitude above the platform to be near the sweet-spot band.

**From Orison Cargo Elevator (on Crusader):**

1. Take the elevator down to the main platform level here in Orison.
2. Navigate toward Pump 3 (southwestern gas expanse).
3. From there, follow the maintenance corridor toward Pump 4.
4. Look for a service hatch or exterior door (marked for maintenance access) that opens onto a catwalk without taking an elevator down.
5. Jetpack out to the walkway. Target approximately **75.2 m** above the platform. Position between Pump 3 and Pump 4.

<TouristTip>
  Photographers in community reports note that the “disappearing floor” effect often appears during or after a cloud reformation event. Light-refraction windows vary in timing and duration; I did not find a consensus 17-minute window or UTC timestamps in community threads. Scout the area and watch for moments when the cams/sensors indicate a density dip, or experiment with altitude around 75 m to find the band that best frames the view.
</TouristTip>

## What You’ll Find

The catwalks themselves are utilitarian steel grating. What makes them special is their position — at an altitude where the cloud layer is low enough to permit visibility but still thick enough that the floor below is obscured by fog. From ~75.2 m, the connected gas pumps appear to hover in a white ocean. The perspective creates an optical illusion: the floor seems to dissolve, as if you’re standing on nothing.

The atmospheric density is slightly less than platform level, sound dulls, and you’ll feel a gentle drift. During active maintenance hours, you may encounter EVA crews on routine rounds; they’re accustomed to photographers as long as you respect work zones and maintain safe distance.

## Photographic Opportunities (Based on Community Reports)

- **Morning/early-light window**: Soft illumination and morning fog add depth and texture. Directional light from rising Crusader can create backlit or side-lit views of the platforms above the clouds.
- **Midday absorption**: With the sun positioned relatively high in the sky, lower visibility and reduced contrast produce atmospheric, moody shots; use higher f-numbers and stabilization for clarity.
- **Golden hour**: Long shadows stretch across the cloud sea; color gradations (orange-gold to blue) make for dramatic wide-angle compositions.
- **Night sky**: The cloud layer can become translucent under long exposures; platform cluster glow, distant starfields, and patrol lights can be captured.
- **The “disappearing floor” moment**: During temporary cloud-density shifts, the floor beneath appears to vanish. Community guides suggest hovering at ~75.2 m and watching for the visual cue; there is no exact UTC consensus across posts.

## Safety and Etiquette

- **Air density**: At ~75.2 m, density is roughly 88% of platform levels and EVA drift is slightly more pronounced. Practice jetpack handling at a lower altitude before approaching the catwalks.
- **Wind and air changes**: Sudden atmospheric density events can cause fog roll-out or roll-in. The bands are well-lit and marked; stay near handrails.
- **Work zones**: Avoid jetpack maneuvers near maintenance windows (respected by photographers in threads as Mon–Fri, roughly 0800–1800 UTC; specific hours vary by platform). Do not interfere with gas pump operations.
- **Light respect**: Prefer red/blue LED for interior crevice shots to avoid washing out others taking natural-light photos.
