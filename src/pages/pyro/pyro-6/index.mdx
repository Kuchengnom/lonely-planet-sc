## The Planet

Pyro VI is a classic gas giant — massive, turbulent, and utterly hostile to anything resembling life. Its atmosphere is a stew of hydrogen, helium, and traces of ammonia, methane, and water vapor. The pressure increases exponentially as you descend, and the temperature ranges from -150°C at the cloud tops to thousands of degrees at the core.

The planet's most striking feature is its storm bands — parallel bands of clouds that move at different speeds, creating spectacular turbulence. The largest of these, the Great Scarlet Spot, is a storm the size of a small moon that has been raging for at least a century. It is visible from the surface of Pyro V on a clear day — a red eye watching the system.

## Highlights

### The Moons of Pyro VI

Pyro VI has a retinue of moons, ranging from captured asteroids to a few small terrestrial bodies. None are particularly hospitable, but some have strategic value. Pyro VIb, for instance, is a small rocky moon with a thin atmosphere and a surface covered in impact craters. It is also the site of a long-abandoned research station, the Pyro VI Orbital Observatory, which was studying the gas giant's storm patterns before funding ran out and the researchers left.

The moons are popular with scavengers and pirates — the former looking for salvage, the latter looking for hideouts. The UEE patrols the area occasionally, but not nearly enough to prevent Pyro VI's moons from being some of the system's most lawless real estate.

### Gas Mining Operations

<AttractionEntry category="hidden-find">
Floating platforms that skim the upper atmosphere, harvesting hydrogen for fuel.

The gas mining platforms of Pyro VI are marvels of engineering — massive structures that float in the planet's upper atmosphere, held aloft by buoyancy and kept in place by powerful thrusters. They harvest hydrogen and helium from the atmosphere, process it into fuel, and ship it throughout the system.

The platforms are dangerous places. The storms are frequent and violent, and a platform that drifts too deep into the atmosphere will be crushed by the pressure. Workers are well-paid for the risk, but turnover is high. Still, for those willing to take the risk, the pay is substantial, and the view — a sea of churning clouds stretching to an impossibly distant horizon — is unlike anything else in the system.

*Insider tip: The platform workers have developed a unique culture, complete with their own slang and a tradition of "storm stories" — increasingly elaborate tales of near-death experiences during particularly violent weather events. The best storytellers are treated like royalty, and a good storm story can get you free drinks at any platform bar.*
</AttractionEntry>

### The Rings

<AttractionEntry category="natural-wonder">
A spectacular ring system composed of ice and rock, lit by the system's red star.

Pyro VI's ring system is one of the most beautiful in the known systems. Composed of ice and rock particles ranging from dust to boulders the size of small ships, the rings stretch for hundreds of thousands of kilometers. In the light of Pyro's red star, they glow with a warm, almost surreal amber hue.

The rings are also dangerous. The particles move at high speed, and a ship that enters the rings without proper shielding will be sandblasted to scrap. But for those who come prepared, the rings are a breathtaking sight — a river of light that circles the planet like a cosmic wedding band.
</AttractionEntry>
