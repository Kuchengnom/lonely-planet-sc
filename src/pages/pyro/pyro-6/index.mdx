Pyro VI is a classic gas giant — massive, turbulent, and utterly hostile to anything resembling life. Its atmosphere is a stew of hydrogen, helium, and traces of ammonia, methane, and water vapor. The pressure increases exponentially as you descend, and the temperature ranges from -150°C at the cloud tops to thousands of degrees at the core. The planet's most striking feature is its storm bands — parallel bands of clouds that move at different speeds, creating spectacular turbulence. The largest, the Great Scarlet Spot, is a storm the size of a small moon that has been raging for at least a century.

### The Moons of Pyro VI

Pyro VI has a retinue of moons, ranging from captured asteroids to a few small terrestrial bodies. None are particularly hospitable, but some have strategic value. Pyro VIb is a small rocky moon with a thin atmosphere and a surface covered in impact craters — and the site of a long-abandoned research station, the Pyro VI Orbital Observatory, which studied the gas giant's storm patterns before funding ran out. The moons are popular with scavengers and pirates: the former looking for salvage, the latter looking for hideouts.

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="Gas Mining Operations" category="hidden-find" image="/images/pyro/pyro-6-mining.webp" imageAlt="Gas mining platforms floating in Pyro VI's upper atmosphere">

The gas mining platforms of Pyro VI are marvels of engineering — massive structures that float in the planet's upper atmosphere, held aloft by buoyancy and kept in place by powerful thrusters. They harvest hydrogen and helium from the atmosphere, process it into fuel, and ship it throughout the system.

The platforms are dangerous places. The storms are frequent and violent, and a platform that drifts too deep into the atmosphere will be crushed by the pressure. Workers are well-paid for the risk, but turnover is high. For those willing to take the risk, the pay is substantial — and the view, a sea of churning clouds stretching to an impossibly distant horizon, is unlike anything else in the system.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Rings" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/pyro/pyro-6-rings.webp" imageAlt="Pyro VI's spectacular ice and rock ring system lit by the red star">

Pyro VI's ring system is one of the most beautiful in the known systems. Composed of ice and rock particles ranging from dust to boulders the size of small ships, the rings stretch for hundreds of thousands of kilometers. In the light of Pyro's red star, they glow with a warm, almost surreal amber hue.

The rings are also dangerous. The particles move at high speed, and a ship that enters the rings without proper shielding will be sandblasted to scrap. But for those who come prepared — parked safely in a Lagrangian point — the rings are a breathtaking sight: a river of light that circles the planet like a cosmic wedding band.

</AttractionEntry>

<TouristTip>

- **Ring Navigation**: If you're flying near the rings for photography, approach at low relative velocity and maintain constant maneuvering thrust. The debris field has no safe corridors — it has only "currently clear" corridors.
- **Platform Access**: Gas mining platforms are private corporate property. Approaching without authorization will get you turned away at best, shot at more likely. Book through the corporate transit office in advance.
- **Moon Salvage**: Pyro VIb and the other moons are technically unclaimed, but established salvage crews have informal territorial arrangements. Announce yourself before landing.
- **Gravity Well**: Pyro VI's gravity well extends far. Plot your exit vector before you arrive — running quantum calculations while falling toward a gas giant is not a situation you want to be in.

</TouristTip>
