Pyro II's surface is a geologist's fever dream. Massive lava plains stretch for hundreds of kilometers, their surfaces cracked and cooled into intricate patterns that look almost deliberate from orbit. Ancient lava tubes snake beneath the surface, some large enough to fly a small ship through. The real danger, though, is the seismic activity — surface quakes are common enough that most outposts are built on shock-absorbing foundations.

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Glass Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/pyro/pyro-2-glass.webp" imageAlt="Vast volcanic glass fields of Pyro II reflecting the red star">

The Glass Fields are exactly what they sound like: vast expanses of volcanic glass, formed when lava flows met ancient lakebeds and cooled instantly. The result is a landscape of shimmering, razor-sharp obsidian that reflects the system's red star in a thousand directions. It is breathtaking and lethal in equal measure.

The glass is sharp enough to shred most EVA suits, and the reflective surface can disorient pilots who try to land without proper terrain mapping. But for those who come prepared, the Glass Fields are one of the most visually stunning landscapes in the system. Some ultra-luxury tourism companies have started offering guided walks across specially treated paths. The locals laugh at this, but they cash the checks.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Spine" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/pyro/pyro-2-spine.webp" imageAlt="Volcanic spire ridge of the Spine cutting across Pyro II's northern hemisphere">

The Spine is a geological anomaly — a chain of volcanic spires that stretches for over 4,000 kilometers across the northern hemisphere. The spires range from a few hundred meters to over two kilometers in height, and their bases are honeycombed with lava tubes and caves. Some of these caves have been expanded by miners and smugglers into makeshift bases and hideouts.

The Spine is also a navigational landmark. Pilots use it to orient themselves when approaching Pyro II, and its distinctive profile is visible from orbit. "Follow the Spine to the Heart" is a common saying among Pyro pilots, referring to the planet's most active volcanic region at the spine's southern terminus. The Spine Ridge habs, built into the rock itself, are the closest thing the planet has to permanent civilization.

</AttractionEntry>

<TouristTip>

- **Glass Field Navigation**: Never attempt to cross the Glass Fields without terrain mapping loaded. The reflective surface creates visual confusion, and a wrong step means shredded suit seals.
- **Seismic Awareness**: Ground quakes are frequent and largely unpredictable. Anchor your ship to hardened basalt, not volcanic soil. What holds you today may crack open tomorrow.
- **The Spine Habs**: Transmit on the independent frequency before approaching Spine Ridge. The hab operators are welcoming, but unannounced arrivals occasionally get shot at by nervous neighbors.
- **Lava Tubes**: Tempting for exploration, genuinely dangerous. Many connect to active magma systems deeper down. If the air smells of sulfur inside a tube, back out immediately.

</TouristTip>
