## The Surface

Pyro II's surface is a geologist's fever dream. Massive lava plains stretch for hundreds of kilometers, their surfaces cracked and cooled into intricate patterns that look almost deliberate from orbit. Ancient lava tubes snake beneath the surface, some large enough to fly a small ship through — though the UEE doesn't recommend it, and neither do we.

The real danger, though, is the seismic activity. The planet's core is restless, and surface quakes are common enough that most outposts are built on shock-absorbing foundations. One mining station, Crimson Hollow, was abandoned after a particularly violent quake cracked its main habitation dome. The station is still there, a skeletal reminder of the planet's indifference to human ambition.

## Attractions

### The Glass Fields

<AttractionEntry category="natural-wonder">
Where lava meets crystal — Pyro II's most beautiful death trap.

The Glass Fields are exactly what they sound like: vast expanses of volcanic glass, formed when lava flows met ancient lakebeds and cooled instantly. The result is a landscape of shimmering, razor-sharp obsidian that reflects the system's red star in a thousand directions. It is breathtaking and lethal in equal measure.

The glass is sharp enough to shred most EVA suits, and the reflective surface can disorient pilots who try to land without proper terrain mapping. But for those who come prepared, the Glass Fields are one of the most visually stunning landscapes in the system. Some ultra-luxury tourism companies have started offering "glass-walk excursions" — guided walks across specially treated paths, complete with champagne service. The locals laugh at this, but they cash the checks.
</AttractionEntry>

### The Spine

<AttractionEntry category="natural-wonder">
A ridge of volcanic spires that cuts across the northern hemisphere like a giant's backbone.

The Spine is a geological anomaly — a chain of volcanic spires that stretches for over 4,000 kilometers. The spires range from a few hundred meters to over two kilometers in height, and their bases are honeycombed with lava tubes and caves. Some of these caves have been expanded by miners and smugglers into makeshift bases and hideouts.

The Spine is also a navigational landmark. Pilots use it to orient themselves when approaching Pyro II, and its distinctive profile is visible from orbit. "Follow the Spine to the Heart" is a common saying among Pyro pilots, referring to the planet's most active volcanic region, which lies at the spine's southern terminus.
</AttractionEntry>
