Pyro I's surface is a study in planetary hostility. The ground is predominantly molten or recently-solidified lava, punctuated by active volcanic vents that belch sulfur dioxide and molten rock into an atmosphere already thick with corrosive compounds. The sky, when visible through the ash clouds, is a bruised purple from the sulfur compounds in the air. The planet is geologically active in a way that makes Cellin look dormant. Volcanic eruptions are not events — they are the background noise of daily existence.

<AttractionEntry number={1} title="The Molten Fields" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/pyro/pyro-1-lava.webp" imageAlt="Rivers of molten lava flowing across Pyro I's surface">

The Molten Fields cover approximately 40% of Pyro I's surface — an endless expanse of slowly moving lava flows that shift and crack like a living thing. The fields are not uniform; they range from slow, viscous flows that creep at a walking pace to fast-moving rivers of liquid rock that can outrun a rover.

The temperature at the surface of the lava ranges from 700°C to 1,200°C, which means standard EVA suits will fail on contact. Specialized mining suits with active cooling and thermal shielding are required, and even those have time limits. Most surface work is done in short shifts — 15 minutes out, 30 minutes in the cooling chamber.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={2} title="The Sulfur Pits" category="natural-wonder" image="/images/pyro/pyro-1-sulfur.webp" imageAlt="Yellow sulfur deposits around volcanic vents">

Pyro I's atmosphere is rich in sulfur compounds, and where that atmosphere meets the cooling lava, it forms deposits of elemental sulfur in colors ranging from pale yellow to deep orange. The Sulfur Pits are vast depressions where volcanic gases have condensed on the crater walls, creating formations that look like something from an artist's fever dream.

The pits are beautiful and lethal. The sulfur is mixed with hydrochloric and sulfuric acid in concentrations that will dissolve unprotected metal in minutes. The miners who work the Sulfur Pits wear suits rated for extreme chemical exposure and carry emergency decontamination kits as standard equipment. Their life expectancy is lower than average. Their pay is higher than average. The math, they say, works out.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={3} title="Independent Mining Claims" category="settlement" image="/images/pyro/pyro-1-claims.webp" imageAlt="A makeshift mining settlement on the edge of a lava flow">

Between the major corporate extraction facilities, in the cracks and crevices of the planet's surface, exist hundreds of independent mining claims staked by freelancers, desperate prospectors, and the occasional criminal enterprise. These claims are not recognized by any government and not on any official map. They exist because someone with a ship and a drill said "this area is mine" and was willing to defend that claim with a plasma rifle.

The claims themselves are makeshift affairs — prefab shelters bolted to the rock, life support systems scavenged from derelict ships, and mining equipment that has been repaired so many times it barely resembles the original design. The people who live here are hard, resourceful, and deeply paranoid. Which is the only reason they're still alive.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={4} title="The Crystal Caves" category="hidden-find" image="/images/pyro/pyro-1-crystal.webp" imageAlt="Massive crystal formations in a volcanic cave">

Deep beneath Pyro I's surface, where the magma has cooled slowly enough to form crystalline structures, exist chambers of staggering beauty. The Crystal Caves are vast underground voids lined with mineral formations that have grown over millennia in stable (by Pyro I standards) conditions, away from the surface chaos.

The crystals glow — not with their own light, but with the reflected light of lava flows that have found their way into the cave systems, creating an effect like standing inside a living chandelier. The mining is done with extreme care — the crystal structures are fragile, and a careless blast can shatter a formation worth more than a small spacecraft.

</AttractionEntry>

<AttractionEntry number={5} title="Extreme Mining Operations" category="practical" image="/images/pyro/pyro-1-mining.webp" imageAlt="Heavy mining equipment operating near a lava flow">

Pyro I's mining industry is not for the faint of heart. The major operations use massive automated machinery — thermal drones, robotic excavators, and chemical extraction facilities — operated remotely from orbit. The only humans on the surface are maintenance crews who venture out in heavily armored suits to repair the machines.

The mining here targets rare volcanic minerals — compounds that form only under extreme temperatures and pressures of active volcanism. These materials are used in everything from high-performance electronics to advanced armor plating. The demand is high, the supply is dangerous, and the miners who provide it are legends in their own right.

</AttractionEntry>

<TouristTip>

- **Thermal Protection**: Standard EVA suits will fail on contact with Pyro I's surface. You need specialized thermal mining suits with active cooling systems. If your suit doesn't look like it belongs on a deep-sea diving expedition, don't get out of the ship.
- **Atmospheric Protection**: The air is a mixture of sulfur dioxide, hydrochloric acid, and other corrosive compounds. Standard filtration won't cut it. You need a full chemical scrubbing system.
- **Emergency Protocols**: If your suit fails, your ship should be within sprinting distance. No exceptions. The groundcrew here doesn't "rescue" people — they recover bodies.
- **Claim Respect**: Independent mining claims are defended fiercely. Do not enter a claim without permission. The claim owner will not ask questions before shooting.
- **Volcanic Activity**: The volcanoes don't announce their eruptions on a schedule. Seismic monitors are your friend. If the ground starts vibrating, get to your ship or the nearest shelter immediately.
- **Medical Facilities**: There are no hospitals on Pyro I. The mining corporations' infirmaries reserve treatment for their own employees. Independent medics exist, but their rates make the danger pay seem reasonable.

</TouristTip>
