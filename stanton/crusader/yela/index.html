Yela

Stanton (Crusader) System · Stanton IV

Best For

Yela doesn't welcome you. It tolerates you. Crusader's oldest moon is a frozen tomb of water-ice and regret, where the silence between ice falls is so complete you can hear your own heartbeat echo in your helmet. And somewhere in that stillness, carved into a hollowed-out asteroid, sits GrimHEX — the system's most notorious outlaw haven. Come for the crystalline caves. Stay because you owe someone money and they know where you sleep.

Sights & Highlights

Vitals

  • Type: Moon (Water-Ice Crust)
  • Orbits: Crusader (Stanton II)
  • Atmosphere: None. Surface is exposed hard vacuum.
  • Average Temperature: -100°C to -80°C
  • Gravity: 0.37 G
  • Day Length: ~27 Standard Hours

Getting Here

Yela doesn’t have a formal spaceport — at least not one that files transit plans with the UEE. Most pilots set down at GrimHEX (technically an asteroid, but it’s the closest thing to civilization for 200,000 klicks). If you’re coming from Orison, it’s a short quantum jump to Crusader’s outer Lagrangian point, then a sub-light burn through the ice field. Bring thermal plating. The moon’s surface radiates cold the way a black hole radiates gravity — it will find every gap in your hull.

Explorer Note

The Landscape

Yela’s surface is a contradiction — brutally hostile and achingly beautiful. Kilometer-thick sheets of water-ice have been compressed over millennia into formations that refract starlight like cathedral glass. In the low-angle light of Crusader’s distant sun, the ice glows with an internal blue-white fire that no photograph has ever adequately captured.

But the beauty is lethal. Yela has no atmosphere to moderate temperature swings. Surface rock and ice are chemically identical after eons of radiation bombardment — both will shatter your landing gear if you touch down on the wrong shelf. The moon’s low gravity means dust and ice particles hang suspended for hours after any disturbance, reducing visibility to zero.

1. GrimHEX

1

GrimHEX

Hidden Find

GrimHEX is not on Yela — it’s near Yela, a former mining asteroid converted into a no-questions-asked waystation for the system’s less… official travelers. The Nine Tails outlaw faction effectively runs the place, though the UEE pretends not to notice.

What makes GrimHEX remarkable isn’t the station itself — it’s the ecosystem. Here you’ll find bounty hunters sharing a bar with their targets, smugglers negotiating prices in the open, and information brokers who’d sell their own mothers for the right price. The station’s interior is a maze of repurposed mining corridors, hydroponic bays that smell inexplicably of rubber and onion, and a central hub where the gravity is just slightly wrong — the kind of wrong that makes you check your drink twice.

The station’s repair facilities are surprisingly good (necessity is the mother of invention, and outlaws break things). The medical bay less so. Approach either with appropriate expectations.

2. The Crystal Caves

2

The Crystal Caves

Natural Wonder

Yela’s most breathtaking secret isn’t on any starmap — it’s beneath the surface. Thermal vents and tidal stress from Crusader have carved a labyrinth of caves through the moon’s ice crust. Some of these chambers are large enough to swallow a Retaliator bomber, their walls lined with ice crystals that grow in geometric impossibilities.

The refractive properties of Yela ice are unlike anything in the known systems. Bring a light source — even a weak one — and the entire chamber comes alive with spectral color. The effect has been described as “standing inside a cut diamond at the edge of a nebula.”

Be cautious, though. The moon’s low gravity means ice shelves can be structurally deceptive. What looks like solid floor might be a thin crust over a thermal vent. The experienced spelunker checks everything twice and carries redundant tethering equipment.

The best caves are on the moon’s dark side, where Crusader’s reflected light doesn’t interfere with the refractive displays. But the dark side is also 30 degrees colder. Choose your experience accordingly.

3. Abandoned Mining Outposts

3

Derelict Mining Outposts

Hidden Find

Yela’s ice isn’t just pretty — it’s rich. Several mining corporations established surface operations in the last century, only to abandon them when extraction costs exceeded projections (or when local “entrepreneurs” made operating conditions difficult). These outposts now stand as frozen monuments to corporate optimism, their equipment locked in eternal stasis.

Some of the larger outposts — particularly those around the Benedra Ice Shelf — still contain operational mining equipment. Pilots with the right technical skills can repurpose laser drills and thermal extractors for their own use. Just be aware that “abandoned” doesn’t mean “unoccupied.” Several outposts have become ad-hoc shelters for travelers who’ve overstayed their welcome elsewhere.

The outposts also make excellent emergency shelters. If your ship develops problems in Yela orbit, an abandoned outpost with intact atmosphere seals is infinitely preferable to trying to repair your ship in hard vacuum.

4. The Crashed Ship (Benedra Region)

4

The Frozen Wreck

Hidden Find

There’s a converted Hull-C cargo vessel crashed on Yela’s southern pole that no one talks about. The UEE hasn’t claimed it. Insurance records are sealed. The ship’s transponder hasn’t broadcast in three years, but thermal imaging shows it’s still warmer than ambient — something inside is generating heat.

Pilots who’ve approached it report finding cargo containers that have been opened methodically, not explosively. The kind of opening that suggests whoever did it knew exactly what they were looking for. Some containers are empty. Others contain items that don’t match the manifest: sealed canisters of indeterminate origin, crates marked only with serial numbers, and one container that, according to witnesses who got close enough, contained something that moved.

The UEE advises all pilots to maintain a 500-meter minimum distance from the wreck for “radiation safety.” Given the lack of radioactive signature, the reason for this advice remains officially unexplained.

5. Yela Surface Traversal

5

Surface Traversal

Getting Around

Getting around Yela’s surface is an exercise in patience and paranoia. The ice crust is geologically active — thermal vents open without warning, and the moon’s low gravity means any vehicle losing traction is likely to bounce rather than slide, which sounds comical until you’re the one bouncing toward a crevasse.

Standard vehicles function adequately on the flatter regions near GrimHEX, but for serious exploration, you’ll want something with ground clearance and redundant heating. The ice sheets near the equator are relatively stable (“relatively” being the operative word), but the polar regions are riddled with pressure ridges and hidden crevasses that can swallow a rover whole.

Most experienced Yela travelers recommend traveling during the moon’s “day” — when Crusader is visible in the sky — not because the light helps (it barely does), but because the slight warming effect reduces the likelihood of sudden ice fractures. Night travel should be avoided unless absolutely necessary, and even then, only with ground-penetrating radar and someone on the comm line who can mount a rescue.

Getting Around

Your primary “spaceport” is GrimHEX. From there, you can commission a shuttle to the surface (most shuttle pilots working the GrimHEX route have seen things you wouldn’t believe, and their rates reflect this). Alternatively, if you’re flying your own ship, the ice fields near GrimHEX offer relatively flat landing zones — just watch for hidden thermal vents.

For surface transport, rovers are available for rent at GrimHEX, though the quality varies from “barely functional” to “this might actually be a repurposed mining cart.” Bring your own repair kit.

💡

  • Survival: Yela’s surface temperature will kill an unprotected human in under a minute. Always maintain suit integrity. Your life support is your religion here — service it accordingly.
  • Communication: Yela’s lack of atmosphere means radio signals propagate well, but the moon’s ionized crust can cause intermittent interference. Always file a flight plan with someone who will miss you.
  • GrimHEX Etiquette: Don’t ask too many questions. Don’t look at anyone’s cargo too carefully. And for the love of all that is holy, do not attempt to enforce UEE law unless you have overwhelming firepower and a death wish.
  • Ice Cave Safety: Never enter alone. The caves are structurally unpredictable, and the low gravity means a fall won’t hurt — but getting stuck in a chimney formation with no one to hear you is effectively a death sentence.
  • Emergency Protocol: If your ship becomes inoperable, GrimHEX is your best bet. They may be outlaws, but they understand the economics of rescue — dead pilots don’t pay docking fees.
